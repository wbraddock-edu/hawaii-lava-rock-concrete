How Volcanic Soil Affects Your Home Foundation

TechnicalMay 5, 2026 · By Derek Makua

Building on Hawaii Island is unlike building anywhere else on earth. The Big Island is geologically young — actively volcanic, seismically active, and built on layers of cooled lava flows that create a uniquely challenging foundation environment. Understanding how volcanic geology affects your home's foundation is essential for any Big Island homeowner or builder.

Lava Tube Risk

Hawaii Island is honeycombed with lava tubes — hollow channels left by flowing lava that has cooled on the outside while continuing to flow inside. Some lava tubes are massive, while others are small. The critical issue for foundations is that building directly above an unknown lava tube creates the risk of collapse under load. Before any significant foundation work, we recommend a professional geotechnical assessment that includes investigation for subsurface voids. This is non-negotiable for us on large projects.

A-Aa vs Pahoehoe Lava

Hawaii has two main types of lava, and they behave very differently as foundation material. A-a (chunky, rough lava) is highly irregular and provides poor bearing support in its upper layers. Pahoehoe (smooth, ropy lava) tends to form more solid, continuous surfaces that can be excellent foundation material when solid. Knowing what you're building on matters enormously for foundation design.

Drainage and Water Table

Volcanic soil is often highly permeable, meaning water drains quickly — which is generally good for foundations. However, in areas with significant rainfall like Kona's windward slopes, water can accumulate in lower lava layers and create unexpected pressure. Proper drainage design is critical for any Big Island foundation.

Our Approach

Every foundation project we take on starts with a thorough site assessment. We core drill when appropriate, consult with geotechnical engineers on larger projects, and design reinforcement and drainage systems specific to each site's conditions.
